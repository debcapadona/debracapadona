What This Is

System Cartographer is a starting point designed to be executed by AI: a structured way for models to begin analyzing enterprise systems, classify decision points, and reason about autonomy, oversight, and constraint—to support the design of next-generation, AI-first systems. It doesn't answer every question. It answers the first one: where do I even start?

What It Does

THREE THINGS AI CAN NOW REASON ABOUT

Building AI-first enterprise systems requires answering questions that existing architecture frameworks don't ask. System Cartographer provides the structure to ask them—and gives AI a repeatable method to answer them.

01
Classify Decision Points
Every system contains decisions. Which ones belong to AI? Which require human judgment? Which are hybrid? System Cartographer gives AI a structured method to classify them— consistently, across any domain.
02
Reason About Autonomy
Not every decision should be autonomous. The framework surfaces the signals—master data dependency, regulatory exposure, reversibility— that determine where autonomy is safe and where it isn't.
03
Define Oversight & Constraint
Where AI acts autonomously, what are the guardrails? Where humans are in the loop, how is that seam designed? System Cartographer structures those answers into architecture decisions.
The Framework

NINE QUESTIONS. ANY SYSTEM.

Building AI-first enterprise systems requires answering questions that existing architecture frameworks don't ask. System Cartographer doesn't answer all of them—but it gives AI a structured place to start. These nine questions emerged from analyzing real systems, not from theory. They're a scaffold, not a prescription.

Q1
What is the decision? Define the discrete choice or output the system produces at this point.
Q2
Who or what currently makes it? Human, algorithm, rule engine, or hybrid—and at what frequency.
Q3
What data does it depend on? Especially: does it depend on master data? Master data dependency is the primary architectural signal.
Q4
What are the consequences of a wrong decision? Reversible vs. irreversible. Financial, regulatory, reputational exposure.
Q5
Is this decision regulated? Explicit regulatory requirements constrain autonomy regardless of AI capability.
Q6
How explainable must the decision be? Audit trail requirements, stakeholder expectations, regulatory disclosure.
Q7
What is the confidence threshold for autonomous action? At what certainty level should AI act vs. escalate to human review?
Q8
Where is the human-in-the-loop seam? If humans must be involved, how is that handoff designed into the system architecture?
Q9
How does this decision interact with others? Upstream and downstream dependencies, cascading effects, system-level risk.
The Output

THREE TIERS OF OWNERSHIP

The nine questions produce a classification. Every decision point in an enterprise system falls into one of three ownership tiers. Designing AI-first systems means deliberately placing each decision—not defaulting.

Autonomy Spectrum
Full AI Autonomy Full Human Ownership
AI Owns
High-frequency, low-consequence, pattern-driven. No regulatory constraint. Reversible. AI acts without human review.
Collaborative
AI recommends with confidence score. Human approves. The seam is an explicit architecture decision, not an afterthought.
Human Owns
Regulated, irreversible, or master-data-dependent at a level requiring human accountability. AI may assist; human decides.

BUILT FOR THE
AI-FIRST ERA

The question is no longer whether AI belongs in enterprise systems. It's how to design systems where AI and humans each own what they're suited to own—and where that boundary is a deliberate architecture decision.

System Cartographer doesn't solve that problem. It gives you a structured place to start reasoning about it—and that turns out to be the hard part.
