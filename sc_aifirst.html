System Cartographer

A starting point designed to be executed by AI: a structured way for models to analyze enterprise systems, classify decision points, and reason about autonomy, oversight, and constraint—to support the design of next-generation, AI-first systems. It doesn't answer every question. It answers the first one: where do I even start?

What This Is — and What It Isn't
A scaffold, not a solution

When I started analyzing enterprise codebases for AI decision points, the first problem was just knowing what to look for. The nine questions here are what emerged from that process. They're not exhaustive—but they gave me a place to start, and that turned out to be the hard part.

What it is not — AI managing master data stores (MDM). That space is mature and well-solved.

What it is — A structured way to reason about where AI autonomy is safe, earnable, or prohibited in a given enterprise system.

01
Classify Decision Points
Which decisions belong to AI? Which require human judgment? System Cartographer gives AI a structured method to classify them—consistently, across any domain.
02
Reason About Autonomy
Master data dependency, regulatory exposure, reversibility—the framework surfaces the signals that determine where autonomy is safe and where it isn't.
03
Define Oversight & Constraint
Where humans must be in the loop, how is that seam designed into the architecture? System Cartographer structures those answers into deliberate decisions.
❓
The Nine Questions
Applied to every decision point extracted from a codebase. Questions are weighted — reversibility and regulatory floor carry the most signal.
Q1
Reversibility Test — What are the consequences and how easily can this be undone? Irreversibility is the single strongest argument for human review.
Q2
Money Movement Test — Does money move as a result of this decision? Financial irreversibility compounds all other risk signals.
Q3 — Regulatory Floor
Regulatory Test — Does law or regulation require a named human in this decision? This is the only permanent wall. Everything else is earnable.
Q4
Volume / Pattern Test — Is this repeated logic with consistent inputs? High volume + consistency = prime AI candidate.
Q5 — Override Tell
Override Signal Test — Is there an "Override" in the command name? That's historical record of where humans didn't trust automation. Pay attention.
Q6
Discretion Test — Does this require unmeasured factors—empathy, relationship context, judgment that defies codification? Lowest AI confidence territory.
Q7
Anomaly Test — Is this detecting a pattern vs. responding to one? Detection is AI-suited. Response to edge-case anomaly requires more caution.
Q8
Cascade Test — What is the downstream impact scope? A decision that triggers a chain of other decisions needs proportionally higher confidence before autonomous execution.
Q9 — Feedback Velocity
Feedback Velocity Test — How fast does feedback become knowable? Fast feedback = faster confidence curve. Velocity determines the shape of the learning curve, not the destination.
📊
Confidence-Based Handoff Model
Every command gets an AI model prediction before execution. Classification is confidence-based with a learning loop. Human becomes cosigner, not sole decision-maker.
✓ Immediate (AI Owns)
Safe to automate from day one. No known regulatory flag. Fast feedback. Fully reversible.
~ Earned (Collaborative)
AI recommends after demonstrated confidence across enterprise decision points. Human approves.
⚠ Human Owns
Regulatory floor, deep discretion, or meaningful downstream cascade. AI provides recommendation only. Human remains accountable signatory.

This Is What I've Found So Far
